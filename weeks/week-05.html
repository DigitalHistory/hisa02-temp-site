Week 5

This week we explore the technologies of daily life and energy. We begin with Technologies of Food, using the pressure cooker as a case study in kitchen innovation. Then we turn to Domestic Technology and the Consumption Junction, examining how household technologies transformed family life and gender roles. Next comes Petroleum, Empire, and the Freedom of the Road, exploring how the automobile and the oil industry reshaped societies and environments. Finally, we consider the ongoing Energy Transition and the question of moving beyond carbon.

You will also have Tutorial 5.

Domestic Technology and The Consumption Junction

We will read a chapter of Ruth Schwartz Cowan’s influential More Work for Mother and explore her concept of “The Consumption Junction.”

Cowan’s central argument challenges common assumptions about household technology: rather than reducing women’s work, many domestic innovations actually increased it, while changing its character. The “consumption junction” refers to the crucial moment when consumers decide whether and how to adopt new technologies.

Key questions for the readings:

  • How did the introduction of gas, electricity, and running water transform domestic life?
  • Why did some labor-saving devices actually create more work for women?
  • How do social expectations and gender roles shape the adoption of domestic technologies?
  • What is the “consumption junction” and why is it important for understanding technological change?

Petroleum, Empire, and the Freedom of the Road

We examine one of the most transformative technologies of the 20th century: the automobile and the petroleum industry that powers it. We will read McCarthy’s account of how the automobile industry developed and transformed American society and environment.

Key questions to consider as you read:

  • How did the automobile industry emerge and what were its early challenges?
  • What role did petroleum play in reshaping global politics and economics?
  • How did cars change patterns of urban and suburban development?
  • What environmental consequences followed from mass automobile adoption?
  • How did the automobile become associated with ideas of freedom and individualism?
  • What groups were excluded from or harmed by automobile-centered development?

Tutorial 5 Preparation

This week’s lectures span a remarkable range of energy and domestic technologies. In tutorial, we will bring them together under the theme of energy and society. Some questions to consider:

  • How do energy systems – coal, petroleum, electricity – shape the organization of daily life? Think about the differences between a coal-heated home, a gas-powered kitchen, and an electrified suburb.
  • Cowan argues that domestic technologies created more work for women, not less. How does this complicate the idea that energy technologies are inherently liberating?
  • The automobile and petroleum reshaped cities, economies, and geopolitics. As we now face an energy transition away from carbon, what can the history of earlier energy transitions teach us? Is there anything in the readings that suggests these transitions are straightforward – or that they are not?
