Week 6

This week we swerve from daily life to the large-scale technologies of warfare and “big science.” We begin with Death from Above: Aerial Bombardment, exploring the ideology of strategic bombing and the experience of being bombed. Then War as Big Tech focusses on the development of “big science” projects in the mid-20th century, particularly the atomic bomb and its legacy. Next, Calculating Machines traces the development of the modern computer industry. Finally, Biology as Big Tech examines how data collection and biological categorization have served as instruments of power.

You will also have Tutorial 6.

Understanding the Readings

Hippler, “Governing from the Skies”

This is one a number of works by the author on the history of strategic bombing. It may be somewhat difficult to understand if you are not already familiar with the major figures Hippler cites. Don’t worry if you don’t understand everything, but make a real effort. Here are some evocative phrases for you to explore:

  • “It was in no way surprising, therefore, that air power should have been viewed as a solution to the war of position of 1914-18.” What is this problem, and how does air power offer a solution to it?”
  • “Designed to restore order, air strikes were no longer a practice of warfare, but rather one of ‘policing’, even ‘imperial policing’: they were practised not within the frontiers of a state, but on a global scale, as a means of governing the world.” What does this mean, and do you agree?
  • “The evolution of air warfare thus reveals to the world the convergence between the ‘advances’ of international law and the pure violence of the state.” This is Hippler’s closing line. What does it mean?

Survivor Accounts

The two survivor accounts give insight into what it was like to be struck by bombs. Read them both carefully.

  • Yoshito Matsushige describes the intensity of the Hiroshima blast. He also describes the surreal and horrific experience of wandering among the dead afterwards. What is your response to this text?
  • Lothar Metzger recounts the horror of the Dresden firebombing. Again, what is your response?
  • In both cases, the bombing forces were members of “the good guys” – the side fighting against fascism. What is your response to the fact that terrible suffering was intentionally inflicted on civilians by the forces universally acknowledged to be on “the right side of history”?

Bombs and Genomes

This is a textbook chapter and so relatively straightforward. Pay particular attention to the phrase “applied science”; what does the author appear to mean by it? Is their use in tension with the way we have talked about technology up to now?

Uninventing the Bomb

This article from the 1990’s may appear rather quaint, but the question of nuclear weapons expertise remains an extremely current one. McKenzie’s proposed solutions for “uninventing” nuclear weapons are quite different than, say, the approach to that problem that Israel & the US have recently taken.

  • What avenues for abolition of nuclear weapons does McKenzie identify? Which of them seem plausible to you?
  • What does “tacit knowledge” mean?
  • Do you think McKenzie would agree with McClellan that contemporary technology is a form of “applied science”?

Calculating Machines

The 1984 Apple Macintosh ad is an early indicator of the ideological context in which personal computers were introduced into the lives of American consumers. Watch the ad, then ask yourself:

  • What do personal computers have to do with resisting authoritarian governance?
  • Suppose you were in charge of crafting a counterargument to the implicit narrative of the ad. What arguments would you make?

The ad contains references to George Orwell’s classic dystopian novel.

Biology as Big Tech

In this lecture, we pick up on our earlier conversation about census-taking and technologies of administration, but now emphasize the exponential increase in data processing power that characterizes the late twentieth and early twenty-first century. Re-read Cohn’s article with fresh eyes: how would you translate his arguments to present-day data collection? How much of his analysis is still current?

Tutorial 6 Preparation

Be prepared to discuss three topics:

  • First, think about the readings on aerial bombardment and nuclear weapons. If nuclear weapons are “technological systems”, is bombardment also such a system? What implications does that have? How can we, as ordinary people, mitigate the harms caused by these weapons and the social structures that surround them? Consider how the technology of warfare has moved from the battlefield to the laboratory and the factory – and what that means for the relationship between war and civilian life.
  • Second, this week traces a path from wartime “big science” (the Manhattan Project, military computing) to the peacetime “big tech” of personal computers and biological data. How does the wartime model of massive, state-funded research shape the technologies we use today? McKenzie asks whether we can “uninvent” nuclear weapons; can we uninvent any technology? What does the Apple Macintosh ad’s vision of liberation through computing look like when set against the history of military-funded research that made personal computers possible?
  • Also, now is the time to think about the final paper.
