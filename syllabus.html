Introduction

We live in a world that has been profoundly altered by technology. Our homes, our work, our relationships, even our bodies all give evidence of our complex historical and cultural relationship with the tools we use. In this course, we examine how technology makes our world by studying pivotal moments in which technology has deeply transformed economic, social, and cultural relationships. We end by considering some of the ways in which contemporary technology is changing how we think and act. Students will explore theories of technological change and apply them to historical and cultural analysis, including an examination of their own experience. We pay particular attention to the ways that information technology both enable and constrain our work as investigators of historical and cultural phenomena, and we make extensive use of tools from the digital humanities to enhance our understanding and abilities.

Course Format

This class includes two two-hour online lectures per week, as well as a tutorial most weeks of the semester. While lectures are recorded, your presence in class is strongly recommended, and comprises part of your participation grade. Tutorials are also mandatory; both attendance and participation at tutorials count towards your grade. Tutorial participation may occasionally include short assignments or in-class work.

Objectives

At the end of this course, you should:

  • understand basic theoretical perspectives on the relationship between technology (on the one hand) and society and culture (on the other). You should be able to formulate criticisms of common frameworks and offer examples of the kind of phenomena that they explain well (and poorly)
  • be conversant with a variety of important moments in the history of technology, and be able to explain the role that technology played in different kinds of historical transformations.
  • be familiar with foundational concepts of cultural studies
  • develop a sophisticated understanding of the contemporary technological and media environment, and how they affect political, social, and cultural phenomena
  • be confident users of introductory tools in the digital humanities

Course Materials

This course has no textbook. Most readings are available through the U of T library system. A small number are made available directly on Quercus. In both cases, links will be provided in the syllabus. However, you may find the following textbook helpful:

For convenience, I also maintain a shared Zotero library, where you can generally find copies of the topical readings (as well as many other sources). You will find it convenient to install Zotero and register with the Zotero online service so that you can access the readings there. You will learn more about Zotero during the semester.

Policies

Accommodations

Students with diverse learning styles and needs are welcome in this course. In particular, if you have a disability/health consideration that may require accommodations, please feel free to approach me and/or the AccessAbility Services Office as soon as possible.

AccessAbility Services staff (located in Rm AA142, Arts and Administration Building) are available by appointment to assess specific needs, provide referrals and arrange appropriate accommodations 416-287-7560 or email ability.utsc@utoronto.ca. The sooner you let us know your needs the quicker we can assist you in achieving your learning goals in this course.

Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion

Diverse backgrounds, embodiments, and experiences are essential to the critical thinking endeavor at the heart of higher education. As always, I expect you to be respectful of the many social and cultural differences among us, which may include, but are not limited to: age, cultural background, disability, ethnicity, technical ability, gender identity and presentation, citizenship and immigration status, national origin, race, religious and political beliefs, sex, sexual orientation, and socioeconomic status. Please talk with me right away if you experience disrespect in this class—from any source, including me—and I will actively work to address the issue.

Academic Integrity

The University treats cases of cheating and plagiarism very seriously. The University of Toronto’s Code of Behaviour on Academic Matters outlines the behaviours that constitute academic dishonesty and the processes for addressing academic offences.

Potential offences in papers and assignments include using someone else’s ideas or words without appropriate acknowledgement, submitting your own work in more than one course without the permission of the instructor, making up sources or facts, obtaining or providing unauthorized assistance on any assignment (including from AI agents).

On tests and exams, cheating includes using or possessing unauthorized aids, looking at someone else’s answers during an exam or test, misrepresenting your identity, or falsifying or altering any documentation required by the University.

University Land Acknowledgement

We meet on a territory whose history stretches back long before European settlement. For thousands of years, the land on which the University finds itself, as well as greater territory stretching northward from the shores of Lake Ontario, has been the traditional land of Indigenous peoples including the Huron-Wendat, the Seneca, and the Mississaugas of the Credit. Today, this meeting place is still the home to many Indigenous people from across Turtle Island, as well as many newcomers from around the world. We are grateful to have the opportunity to work here, and acknowledge the long history of injustice and expropriation which has brought us to this moment in time.

Use of Generative AI Tools

Students may not use artificial intelligence tools for taking tests, writing research papers, or completing major course assignments, except when the professor has explicitly given guidance on the appropriate role of generative AI in that assignment. These tools may be useful when gathering information from across sources and assimilating it for understanding. Each assignment includes detailed instructions regarding the use of generative AI tools. Whenever such tools are used, that use must be acknowledged and described in detail.

The knowing use of generative artificial intelligence tools, including ChatGPT and other AI writing and coding assistants, for the completion of, or to support the completion of, an examination, term test, assignment, or any other form of academic assessment, may be considered an academic offense in this course when that use contravenes my instructions.

Attendance

Make every effort to attend each class meeting! Class will begin and end on time. Please do your best to arrive before the start of the session. Students are expected to attend all meetings unless unavoidable circumstances prevent you. Please let me know in advance if you need to skip class either once or in general. I may ask you to make up your attendance mark with extra work.

Please silence notifications on your devices during class, so you can focus. Please don’t use class time (lecture or lab) to check your email, update your status, read reddit, watch YouTube, make dank memes, seize the means of production, etc. Such usage is distracting and interferes with learning both for you and for all the other students around you. If I notice you doing this, I will call you out in class. Spend class time on class materials. If another student’s activity is distracting, please ask them to stop it (or let me know outside of class).

Plagiarism Detection Tool

Normally, students will be required to submit their course essays to the University’s plagiarism detection tool for a review of textual similarity and detection of possible plagiarism. In doing so, students will allow their essays to be included as source documents in the tool’s reference database, where they will be used solely for the purpose of detecting plagiarism. The terms that apply to the University’s use of this tool are described on the Centre for Teaching Support & Innovation web site (https://uoft.me/pdt-faq).

Missed Deadlines or Exams

Life happens. If illness, family emergency, or other serious circumstances prevent you from meeting a deadline or writing the midterm, contact me (or your TA, for assignments) as soon as possible — ideally before the deadline. Do not wait until after the fact.

Assignments. Requests for extensions should be made in advance where possible. Short extensions (1–3 days) can usually be arranged without formal documentation if you reach out promptly. Longer extensions may require supporting documentation (see below). Work submitted late without an approved extension is subject to the 5%/calendar-day late penalty described below.

Declaring an Absence. For a missed assignment deadline or the midterm, your first step is normally to file an Absence Declaration on ACORN. Students may use the ACORN Absence Declaration tool once per academic term to declare an absence of up to seven consecutive days without submitting documentation. See the UTSC Registrar’s Absence Declaration page for details. You must also notify me by email within 48 hours so we can make a plan.

If you have already used your ACORN Absence Declaration for the term, or if your circumstances fall outside what ACORN covers, you may be asked to submit a Verification of Illness or Injury (VOI) form or other appropriate documentation.

Midterm. The midterm is written in class on May 27, 2026 (Wed). If you miss it with an approved absence, the weight of the midterm will normally be shifted to the final exam; a make-up midterm will not typically be offered.

Final Exam. Missed final exams are handled through the Office of the Registrar’s deferred exam process, not by the instructor. If you miss the final, you must petition the Registrar for a deferred exam sitting.

Accommodated students. Students registered with AccessAbility Services should arrange test and exam accommodations through that office following the usual procedures; please also let me know in advance.

Marking Scheme

In this large lecture-based class, most of your mark is determined by two synchronous, time-limited exams: A midterm in the fourth week, and a final during exam period in late June. In addition, there are three short writing assignments, the last of which is slightly longer and more complex than the first two. More details on the assignments will be forthcoming as the course progresses. Finally, attendance at both lecture and tutorial, and participation in the latter, will be counted toward your grade.

Handing in assignments late creates difficulties for instructors and, more importantly, often initiates a cascade of difficulties for the student as well. Late work will generally be penalized at a rate of 5%/calendar day. If special circumstances make it impossible for you to hand your work in on time, tell your TA as promptly as possible so that we can create an appropriate plan.
